The visual language of “pig”

14C

  • The first true piggy banks were orange-colored clay jars that had a slot in the top for depositing coins. These jars were made out of an economical material called pygg, and were shaped in round forms that resemble that of a pig.

16C

  • “Pig” began being used as a derogatory term for people others didn't like.

1811

  • The Oxford English Dictionary cites an 1811 reference to a “pig” as a Bow Street Runner—a policeman from London’s First professional police form.

1920

  • Porky is a cartoon character from a 1920 Soviet agitprop poster. In the poster, the character has been used to represent the bourgeois or ruling class, a literal capitalist pig.

1945

  • The allegorical novel Animal Farm by George Orwell depicted pigs as corrupt, venal, drunken, violent.

1960

  • Many younger people began to show deep distrust of police, and terms such as “fuzz” and “pig” reappeared as derogatory nicknames for police. The distrust came from fear of police brutality during political protests and from police corruption.
  • A male chauvinist pig (MCP) was a term used among some feminists typically for men with some power and believed that men were superior.

1967

  • After Eldridge Cleaver took over as Black Panther Minister of Information in mid-1967, the newspaper began to print images specifically intended to incite controversy. Huey P. Newton said “[they] thought up new terms for them. At first I figured that the reverse of god—dog—would be a good epithet, but it did not catch on. [They] tried beast, brute, and animal, but none of them captured the essential quality [they] were trying to convey… Eldridge showed us a postcard from Beverly Axelrod. On the front was the slogan “Support Your Local Police”; there was a sheriff’s star above the phrase, and in the center of the star a grinning, slobbering pig. It was just what [they] were looking for. [They] began to show policemen as pigs in our cartoons, and from time to time used the word. ‘Pig’ caught on and it entered the language.”

1968

  • Pigasus was a 145-pound domestic pig who was nominated for President of the United States as a theatrical gesture by the Youth International Party on August 23, 1968. Yippies were known for using dramatic theatrics in their demonstrations, and they used Pigasus as a way to mock the social status quo. When police disrupted their demonstration, they started to call the police pigs.

1977

  • “Pigs (Three Different Ones)” is a song from Pink Floyd’s 1977 album Animals. In the album’s three parts, “Dogs”, “Pigs” and “Sheep”, pigs represent the people whom Roger Waters considers to be at the top of the social ladder, the ones with wealth and power. He said that pigs manipulate the rest of society and encourage them to be viciously competitive and cutthroat, so that pigs can remain powerful.

1977

  • The original Pink Floyd pig was designed by Roger Waters and built in December 1976 by the artist Jeffrey Shaw with help of design team Hipgnosis, in preparation for shooting the cover of the Animals album. The helium-filled balloon was supposed to remain hovering over Battersea Power Station, but broke free due to a strong gust of wind. It gained a lot of media attention.

2010

  • Between 2014 and 2015, members of the 8chan messageboard /leftypol/ began using Porky as a symbol for capitalist actors, such as, bosses, CEOs, and politicians.

2006

  • Pigs were depicted in the dystopian science-fiction thriller Children of Men (2006), and in an episode of the Simpsons.

2007

  • During a Roger Waters Band performance at the Milwaukee Summerfest, an inflated pig was spotted with the words “Impeach Bush Now” on its butt.

2012

  • Inmates who are tasked to print decals sneak in a pig-shaped cow spot on police cars.
